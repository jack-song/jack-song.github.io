Shock

Now I know a little more about making jumps and taking hard landings.

“It’s definitely overwhelming” - assurances came from many of the people I’ve met. They’re consistently impressed that I’m so new to the African continent. Expats and locals are the same. They’re excited for my stay here, and equally sympathetic about the culture shock.

I landed after a 24 hour flight iterary carrying my 50L backpack. I said no to a border official asking for a tip. I couldn’t tell if it was supposed to be a bribe and he didn’t even fill the form in correctly. I rushed into an Uber because the driver was yelling “faster, faster!” He laughed as we sped away. He said that a policeman was walking over, likely looking for trouble. While I unpack at my new home I realize I’ve caught a cold. The jet lag didn’t help.

I’d like to think I’ve had it rough but it hasn’t been too bad, just unexpected.

Culture shock is real, and things are very different around here. There are power outages. There’s a drought - water outages. The faces I see everywhere are what not what I’m used to. Private property is always gated and every mall enforces security checks. Doctors have been on a nation-wide strike for over a month now. These things reminded me that I was travelling and I took them in stride. Because I like travelling.

It’s the smaller things that tired me out. Being out of breath at the top of a staircase because it’s 1,600m above sea level. Wondering if the way I say “mambo” or “asante” sound empty. Supressing my feelings of concern every time a soldier walks by with a rifle. These small things made me anxious for the next day. The anxiety was kind of fun, but this is not a vacation getaway, it’s a 4 month stay.

More than usual, I’d want to spend some weekend days at home, doing nothing.

I knew that I would get used to it eventually. It felt like waiting for the day you finally feel better after being sick. I also knew that it could take a very long time, or never quite finish completely.

I don’t know when, but I think I’ve already gotten used to most of it. I also realized that the expats I meet have already gone though a similar process, or are currently working through it. Some could be caught in much more difficult circumstances than what I’ve seen. Still, we probably have it relatively easy.

I admire the millions of immigrants around the world going through this process right now; they have bet their livelihoods on it. They’re in a gamble to write personal legends. Stakes are high and enduring the shock of a new world is only a small part of the challenge.
