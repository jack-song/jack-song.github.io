Shock

Now I know a little more about making jumps and taking hard landings.

“It’s overwhelming” - assurances from many of the people I’ve met. They’re consistently impressed that I’m so new to the African continent. Expats and locals are the same. They’re excited for my stay here, and equally sympathetic about the culture shock.

I came in hot, finishing a 24 hour flight iterary with my four-month-stay backpack. I said no to a border official asking for a tip (bribe? he didn’t even fill the form in correctly). I jumped really fast into an Uber because the driver was yelling “faster, faster!” He laughed as we sped away. He says that a policeman was coming, looking for trouble. While I unpack at my new home I realize I’m sick. The jet lag didn’t help.

I’d like to think I’ve had it rough but it hasn’t been too bad, just unexpected.

Culture shock is real, and things are very different around here. There are power outages. There’s a drought - water outages. The faces I see everywhere and every day are what not what I’m used to. I pass through a security check for every property I step onto. Doctors have been on a nation-wide strike for over a month now. These things told me that I was travelling and I took them in stride. Because I like travelling.

It’s the smaller things that tired me out. Being out of breath at the top of a staircase because it’s 1,600m above sea level. Wondering if the way I say “mambo” or “asante” sound empty. Containing my surprise every time I see a soldier with a rifle. These made the whole experience feel fresh and also made me anxious for the next day. The anxiety was kind of fun, but this is not a vacation getaway, or “travelling”. I don’t even have a flight out yet.

More than usual, I’d want to spend some weekend days at home, doing nothing.

I knew that I would get used to all of it. It felt like waiting for the day you feel good after being sick in bed. For me, it’s already happened, or happening, but I can’t explain how or when. And I feel like I’ve finally had a taste of what millions of people have bet their lives on. To write their own personal legends, some are willing to endure the shock of a new world.
